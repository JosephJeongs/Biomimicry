Aircrafts–whether it be passenger, cargo, military–are a great feat in engineering history, additionally inspired by biomimicry. With such a complex design and numerous technical components, there are almost always features that could be redesigned in order to be more efficient, such as in cost or energy usage.

Visualize the last time that you may have sat in an airplane, specifically a seat near the roaring engines. Seconds after the plane touches down onto the runway, your ears are greeted with a rapidly increasing decibel (measurement of sound) level. That is the engines engaging in a mode referred to as “reverse thrust.” In this mode, the airflow from the front to the back of the engine is disrupted and redirected to exit via the sides that open up. This forced airflow then begins to push air to the sides of the engine, including towards the passengers.

So what? Depending on the speed of the touchdown and weight of the aircraft, the reverse thrust is only engaged for tens of seconds, not too bad right? Well, the events that lead up to the engagement of reverse thrusts goes back all the way to the preparation of the flight.

Every aircraft that has the intention to take flight must follow a fuel policy. This simply states that an aircraft should have enough fuel that by the time it arrives at its destination, it is left with almost no fuel. This is critical because of 2 factors of fuel: cost and weight. First, fuel is abundant in aircrafts, meaning that it costs thousands of dollars to operate the flight. Any cutdown on fuel is highly beneficial. Second, fuel holds weight. Tens or hundreds of thousands of pounds of fuel can limit mobility. Additionally, extra weight on aircrafts makes it more vulnerable for rougher landings, discomforting passengers and, more importantly, reducing the life longevity of landing gear (wheels of the aircraft).

With all of this considered, eliminating the need for reverse thrusts can provide numerous valuable benefits. But what could be used as an alternative?

My Design (From Nature)

As previously mentioned, aircrafts, with such a complex design and numerous technical components, are sensitively prone to improvements. In this case, fuel efficiency – and on a more specific scale, reverse thrusts. This is simply redirecting the airflow to the sides of the engine in order to reduce aircraft speed at the expense of some fuel. On dry pavement, this contributes a minimal amount of stopping force. In order to eliminate this unnecessary fuel consumption, a possible proposition is a complete new design of the wings in order for it to create a barrier of surface area against wind. Geese are behind this central idea. Unlike many birds that take flight, geese are particularly fascinating because of how they land in a pond: spreading their wings, they rotate the wall of feathers in order to maximize drag for a slower approach and elegantly take their spots on the water. This “behavior” can be mimicked on planes, albeit less elegantly, with a few approaches. First, this new system can be implemented directly in the leading edge slat, a retractable component on the front of wings, with two large panels that can extend in order to form a V-shape, hypothetically mimicking the drag effect on geese. Alternatively, the leading edge slat can be separated via a horizontal split in order for both components to independently alter their position to form the shape V formation (this mechanism will be referred to as V-force).

However, other methods of deceleration exist, such as parachutes – seen in drag cars and space shuttles. Although they may be exceptional in their purpose, using this method is highly impractical for airplanes as one, there are many flights in a day, meaning a constant reset of the parachutes plus thorough maintenance, and two, cost.

Inventions that come easily often overlook potential problems and this is no exception. V-force is not an implemented mechanism in all aircrafts and if it proves to be effective, then all aircrafts will slowly have to be called back for modifications. Until then, fuel savings will not deem significant and will take a while to reach that potential. Additionally, V-force will not provide an as effective method in braking during wet conditions to reverse thrusts. Luckily, most landings are in the general range of dry conditions and will not require support from reverse thrusts.
